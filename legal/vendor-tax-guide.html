1. Am I Selling Casually or Trading?

HMRC distinguishes between two types of sellers, and which one applies to you determines your tax obligations.

Casual Seller — You are selling personal items you no longer need — clothes, furniture, electronics you previously owned. This is generally not considered trading and may not be taxable. However, the £1,000 trading allowance described below still applies to your gross income.

Trader — You are buying items specifically to resell, selling regularly, or running it as a business. HMRC considers this trading, and your profits are taxable.

The key question HMRC asks is: are you buying and selling with the intention of making a profit? If yes, you are trading.

2. The Trading Allowance — £1,000/year

Every individual in the UK gets a £1,000 tax-free trading allowance per tax year (6 April to 5 April).

  • If your total gross income from selling across all platforms is under £1,000 — you don't need to do anything. No tax, no declaration.
  • If your gross income is over £1,000 — you must register for Self Assessment with HMRC and declare your income.
  • The allowance applies to gross income (before fees), not your profit.

Example: You sell £1,200 worth of goods. Platform fees leave you with £1,050. You still owe tax because your gross was over £1,000.

3. Income Tax on Trading Profits

If you exceed the £1,000 allowance, you pay Income Tax on your profit — not your total sales.

Profit = Total Sales − Cost of Goods − Allowable Expenses

Allowable expenses you can deduct include:

  • Cost of items purchased to resell
  • Packaging and postage costs
  • Platform fees (Sell4Life commission)
  • Stripe / payment processing fees
  • A portion of phone/internet costs if used for the business
  • Mileage if driving to post items

Tax rates 2025/2026

IncomeRate
Up to £12,5700% (Personal Allowance)
£12,571 — £50,27020% Basic Rate
£50,271 — £125,14040% Higher Rate
Over £125,14045% Additional Rate

This is on top of any other income you earn (employment, pension etc). Your total income determines your tax band.

Example: You earn £30,000 from your job and £5,000 profit from selling. Your total income is £35,000 — all within the 20% band. You pay 20% on the £5,000 profit = £1,000 tax.

4. National Insurance Contributions

If HMRC considers you self-employed or trading:

  • Class 2 NI — £3.45/week (if profits over £12,570)
  • Class 4 NI — 9% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270
  • Class 4 NI — 2% on profits above £50,270

Casual sellers who stay under the trading allowance generally don't pay NI.

5. VAT (Value Added Tax)

You only need to register for VAT if your taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in any 12-month rolling period.

  • Below £90,000 — no VAT obligation
  • Above £90,000 — must register with HMRC and charge 20% VAT on sales
  • Once registered, you can also reclaim VAT on business purchases

Most small sellers on Sell4Life will never reach this threshold.

6. Selling Personal Possessions — Capital Gains Tax

If you sell a personal possession (not bought to resell) for over £6,000, you may owe Capital Gains Tax.

  • Rate: 18% basic rate or 24% higher rate taxpayers
  • Annual CGT allowance of £3,000 (2025/2026)
  • Items like cars are exempt

This rarely applies to typical marketplace sellers.

7. Registering with HMRC

If your gross selling income exceeds £1,000 in a tax year you must:

  1. Register for Self Assessment at gov.uk
  2. Complete a tax return by 31 January each year
  3. Pay any tax owed by 31 January
  4. Keep records of all sales, costs and expenses for at least 5 years

Register here: www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment

8. Sell4Life's Role in Reporting

Sell4Life is required by HMRC under the Digital Platform Reporting Rules (effective January 2024) to collect and report seller information to HMRC if you:

  • Make 30 or more sales per year, OR
  • Earn €2,000 (approx £1,700) or more per year on the platform

This means HMRC may already know about your selling activity. It is always better to declare voluntarily than wait for HMRC to contact you.

9. Multiple Platforms

If you sell on multiple platforms (eBay, Vinted, Facebook Marketplace, Sell4Life etc) HMRC looks at your combined income across all platforms — not each one separately.

  • £800 on eBay + £400 on Sell4Life = £1,200 total — over the allowance
  • Keep records of all platforms separately
  • All platforms are now required to report your earnings to HMRC

10. Business Bank Account

If you are trading regularly, HMRC recommends keeping business money separate from personal money.

  • Open a dedicated bank account for selling income
  • Makes it much easier to calculate profit and expenses
  • Shows HMRC you are organised if ever investigated
  • Some banks offer free business accounts for sole traders — Starling, Monzo, Tide

11. Sole Trader vs Limited Company

Most small sellers start as Sole Traders — the simplest option:

  • No registration needed beyond Self Assessment
  • You and the business are legally the same
  • Personally liable for any debts

As your business grows you may consider a Limited Company:

  • Legally separate from you personally
  • Pay Corporation Tax (25%) instead of Income Tax
  • More admin and accountancy costs
  • Protects personal assets if business fails
  • Generally worth considering above £30,000–£50,000 profit

12. Keeping Records

HMRC requires you to keep records for at least 5 years after the January deadline. Records should include:

  • All sales transactions (Sell4Life provides CSV export for this)
  • Purchase receipts for items you bought to resell
  • Postage and packaging receipts
  • Any business expenses
  • Bank statements

Tip: Use Sell4Life's Export CSV feature in your Transactions page to download your full transaction history for your records.

13. Payments on Account

Once registered for Self Assessment, if your tax bill exceeds £1,000, HMRC requires you to make advance payments called Payments on Account:

  • First payment: 31 January (same time as tax return)
  • Second payment: 31 July
  • Each payment is 50% of your previous year's tax bill
  • This catches many new sellers by surprise — be prepared

Example: Your first year tax bill is £2,000. You pay £2,000 in January PLUS £1,000 advance for next year. Total £3,000 due in January.

14. Losses

If you make a loss in a tax year (costs exceed income):

  • You can carry the loss forward to offset future profits
  • You can offset losses against other income in some cases
  • Always declare losses — they work in your favour

15. Gifts and Second-Hand Personal Items

Gifts received and sold: If someone gives you something and you sell it, the full sale price may be taxable as it cost you nothing.

Inherited items: Generally exempt if selling inherited personal possessions under £6,000. Above £6,000, Capital Gains Tax may apply based on value at time of inheritance.

Car boot and charity shop finds: Bought cheaply and resold for profit — this is trading, and fully taxable.

16. HMRC Investigations

HMRC has sophisticated software that monitors online selling activity. They can and do investigate sellers who:

  • Are reported by platforms (mandatory since 2024)
  • Show lifestyle inconsistent with declared income
  • Have unexplained cash deposits
  • Fail to declare when nudge letters are sent

HMRC Nudge Letters

HMRC may send you a letter asking you to check your tax affairs. Do not ignore these. Respond promptly.

Penalties for non-declaration

  • Unprompted disclosure — 0–30% penalty on unpaid tax
  • Prompted disclosure — 15–30% penalty
  • Deliberate non-disclosure — up to 100% penalty plus interest

17. Making Tax Digital (MTD)

HMRC is rolling out Making Tax Digital — requiring self-employed people to submit tax information quarterly through approved software instead of one annual return.

  • From April 2026 — applies to self-employed with income over £50,000
  • From April 2027 — applies to income over £30,000
  • From April 2028 — applies to income over £20,000

If you are a regular seller you may need to use approved accounting software like QuickBooks, Xero, or FreeAgent in the near future.

18. Help and Professional Advice

Consider using a professional if:

  • Your selling income exceeds £5,000 profit per year
  • You are unsure about your tax status
  • You receive a letter from HMRC
  • You want to set up a limited company

Low cost options

  • TaxAid — free tax advice for people on low incomes: taxaid.org.uk
  • HMRC Webchat — gov.uk/contact-hmrc
  • Low cost accountants — many offer fixed-fee self assessment from £150–£300/year
  • Citizens Advice — citizensadvice.org.uk

20. Non-UK Residents Selling on Sell4Life

If you live outside the UK but sell to UK buyers:

  • UK tax rules may still apply depending on where the business activity takes place
  • You may have tax obligations in your own country AND the UK
  • Seek specialist international tax advice
  • Sell4Life may be required to withhold tax in some circumstances
